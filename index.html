About Me

My name is Andrew Hill and I currently work as a research assistant and software developer in Daniel MacArthur's group in the Analytic and Translational Genetics Unit at Massachusetts General Hospital and the Broad Institute in Boston. In September 2014, I will be returning to Seattle as a graduate student in the University of Washington Department of Genome Sciences.

Be sure to check out my blog, projects, and other social media profiles.

Interests

Broadly, I am interested in development of new technologies and computational approaches within genomics. Specifically, I am interested in novel applications of next generation sequencing technologies, particularly clinical applications of sequencing. I also have an interest in Mendelian disease research and loss-of-function variation.

Big Data

Scientific and clinical data are being generated in massive quantities. Effective data visualizations and computational techniques are critical as the pace of data generation continues to accelerate.

Machine Learning

Machine learning algorithms have proven to be very powerful tools in various fields of computational research. I am interested in applications of machine learning within genomics.

Software Tools

As a software developer, I am interested in developing better tools and platforms that clinicians and researchers can apply within their own research.

Variant Annotation

I am interested in developing better tools and methods for more comprehensive and robust functional annotation of variants, particularly loss-of-function variants.

Data Integration

The data generated by researchers and clinicians are highly heterogeneous, but are, in many cases, potentially powerful when used in combination. I am interested in research that seeks to leverage heterogenous data sources.

Personalized Medicine

The dropping price of sequencing is moving to make clinical applications of exome and genome sequencing more widely viable. I am interested technologies and research that enables accurate and robust clinical interpretation of genetic variation.
