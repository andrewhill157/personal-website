About Me

My name is Andrew Hill and I am currently a graduate student in the UW Department of Genome Sciences in Seattle, WA. I am rotating with Jay Shendure, Bill Noble, and Cole Trapnell.

Prior to coming to UW, I also worked as a research assistant and software developer in Daniel MacArthur's group in the Analytic and Translational Genetics Unit at Massachusetts General Hospital and the Broad Institute in Boston, MA.

Interests

Broadly, I am passionate about new technologies and methods in genomics. Specifically, I am currently very interested in novel applications of next generation sequencing technologies to examine problems in areas such as gene regulation, 3D genome architecture, cell fate decisions, clinical diagnostics, and functional interpretation of genetic variation. I also have an strong interest in Mendelian disease, population-scale genome sequencing datasets, and loss-of-function variation.

While my current skill set is very much computational, I am beginning to expand towards adding experimental work to my own projects.

Variant Annotation

I am interested in better tools and methods for more comprehensive and robust functional annotation of genetic variation. This spans computational methods like CADD as well as functional assays like Saturation Mutagenesis.

Genome Architecture

Chromosome Conformation Capture techniques such as Hi-C are enabling more detailed querying of 3D genome architecture. I am interested in more advanced chromosome conformation capture technologies and uses of such data as it relates to gene regulation.

Clinical Genomics

The dropping price of sequencing is moving to make clinical applications of exome and genome sequencing more widely viable. I am interested in applications of sequencing technologies that enable novel diagnostics and robust clinical interpretation of genetic variation.

Infrastructure

Scientific and clinical data are being generated in massive quantities. Effective infrastructure and algorithms that allow for rapid data visualizations and computation over large volumes of data are critical as the pace of data generation continues to accelerate.

Machine Learning

Machine learning algorithms have proven to be very powerful tools in various fields of research. I am interested in applications of machine learning within genomics and have contributed to projects like CADD in the Shendure Lab.

Software Tools

As an engineer, I am interested in developing robust tools and platforms that clinicians and researchers can apply within their own research.
