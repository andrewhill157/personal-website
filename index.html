Interests

Broadly, I am interested in the application of computation to biomedical research problems. I seek to advance our understanding of and ability to treat human disease. Additionally, I am interested in the well-planned application of technology to enhance the quality of healthcare.

Big Data

Scientific and clinical data are being collected in massive quantities. Effective data visualizations and analytics are helping to guide scientific research and inform clinicians. I am interested in researching new ways we can use this data.

Machine Learning

Machine learning algorithms have substantially advanced many areas of computing. The amount of scientific data available is allowing these algorithms to be applied to complex problems in biomedical research.

Software Tools

Software tools are playing a critical role in making powerful and robust computational techniques to the research community. I am interested in helping to develop better tools for computational biology and genomics.

Genetic Variation

While identification and classification of genetic variants has advanced significantly, more work needs to be done in creating more robust tools variant calling and analysis in genomics.

Data Integration

I am excited about research that integrates genomic data, such as variants identified by GWAS, with other data sources, such as epigenetic data, tissue expression data, and protein interaction networks to elucidate genomic regulation and identify new targetable pathways implicated in human disease.

Personalized Medicine

I am also interested in integration of multiple data sources to advance clinical decision making and move towards the goal of personalized medicine.

Research

The following are selected examples of my research projects.

MacArthur Lab of MGH and the Broad Institute of Harvard and MIT

The MacArthur Lab focuses on extracting useful information from human genome sequences. Some of the main focuses of the lab are accurate identification of sequence variants from massive data-sets, identifying LoF variants, using transcriptome sequencing approches to characterize the impact of DNA sequence variants on human gene function, and using genomic approaches to discover disease-causing mutations in severe disease patients.

Leiden Muscular Dystrophy Variant Database Cleanup

I have created a Python script and OpenRefine recipe to automate data extraction and cleanup from the Leiden muscular dystrophy gene variant databases. I have also converted variants given in HGVS notation (relative to cDNA transcripts) to genomic coordinates and validated the results via annotation in order to facilitate use in large-scale data analytics efforts.

University of Washington Biorobotics Lab

The mission of the UW Biorobotics Lab (BRL) is to develop science, technology, and human resources at the interface between robotics and biological movement systems. The two main focuses of the lab during my time there were robotic surgery and novel haptic interfaces.

-
Online Modeling of In Vivo Mechanical Properties of Soft Tissue for Robotic Surgery

I sought to develop an approach to characterize the mechanical properties of soft tissue in real time. Identification of these tissue-specific properties could enable partial-automation of grasping in robotic surgery, preventing unintended damage, and be used to identify different types of healthy and pathological tissue using machine learning algorithms. To address this problem, I designed, built, and programmed a robotic device to collect force and tissue displacement data as it palpated soft-tissue. I used an Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF), a non-linear, recursive filtering algorithm, to identify parameters of several non-linear, dynamic models of soft-tissue from the collected data. I chose the UKF because it allows for simultaneous online identification of tissue model parameters and states of a dynamic system (robot position). I tested and validated system performance on synthetic tissue and then acquired data from in vivo porcine abdominal organs for future analysis. This highly interdisciplinary project allowed me to hone my skills in advanced signal processing/data analysis algorithms as well as embedded system programming and hardware development. I won a Mary Gates Research Scholarship for my project and presented my work at the 2012 UW Undergraduate Research Symposium.

Haptic-Enabled Glove for Musical Performance

Two graduate students and I developed a glove that allows a user to manipulate sound effects playing in the background by moving their hand in space and bending their fingers as if they were playing an instrument. I co-developed the glove hardware and microcontroller firmware to track finger bending using piezoresistive sensors, actuate vibrating motors with increasing finger bending, and relay sensor data via Bluetooth to a computer. We used a Microsoft Kinect to track the user’s hand position, allowing them to control different sound effects in different locations. As a group, we integrated the data in a music synthesis platform to generate sound effects and vary their volume, speed, and pitch with finger bending. Our project has since been extended in the BRL as a general-purpose device for haptic interaction with virtual environments.
